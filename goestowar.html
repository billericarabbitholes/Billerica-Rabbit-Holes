Billerica Goes to War

The 1911 War Game

BOSTON, July 22 — “A foreign power has already obtained possession of the Atlantic Coast, including Boston.”

Reports said the army sent to take it back would form “at Billerica, just south of Lowell.” By the following day, Brig. Gen. E. P. Clark had established headquarters at North Billerica, with thousands of men gathering around him. Tents went up before nightfall. Cavalry horses stood ready to move.

Sound far-fetched? Wondering what part of Billerica history you missed?

You did not miss a battle. You found a massive war game.

In July 1911, the Massachusetts National Guard staged a major military exercise across Middlesex and Essex Counties. The premise was dramatic: a foreign enemy, represented by the “Red” army, had captured Boston and the Atlantic seaboard. The “Blue” army, representing American forces, gathered to take it back. When the mock war opened, Billerica became the Blue Army’s headquarters and rendezvous point.

It was a fake war, but not a fake event. And this was no small local drill.

The New York Times followed the maneuvers daily, the Lowell Courier-Citizen covered them like a field campaign, and the Boston Globe mapped the “scene of war game” across eastern Massachusetts. Governor Eugene Foss, commander-in-chief of the Massachusetts National Guard, visited the soldiers in the field. Newspapers even rumored that President Taft might come to see it himself.

The Blue Army was described as camped “in the fields just below Billerica Centre,” near Brown’s Corner, along the road from North Billerica to the Centre. What had been farmland became an armed camp almost overnight.

For two nights in July 1911, Billerica likely hosted the largest military force ever assembled on its soil.

That scale is hard to overstate. Billerica’s population in 1910 was only about 2,800, perhaps roughly 3,000 by 1911. Contemporary accounts placed the Blue Army at several thousand men, with some reports as high as 8,000.

Then came the spectators. On Sunday, the Globe estimated that at least 10,000 visitors came to the camp. They came to watch troops arrive and depart, see camp made and broken, hear band concerts, and more.

If the opening sounds older than 1911, that is partly because cavalry, tents, and field headquarters still belonged to the military world people expected to see. But this was not simply an old-fashioned encampment. It was also a test of newer military methods and technologies. Reports describe motor trucks replacing older wagon trains, officers using automobiles, scouts on motorcycles, wireless equipment in the field, and regular army observers judging the work. One account even noted that “all the resources of modern warfare” would be used, except the “aeroplane” — not because aircraft were unimaginable, but because the countryside was considered unsuitable for their operation.

The photographs can make the whole thing look almost festive, and in Billerica it seems to have been. And why not? The troops had just arrived. Tents were going up. Visitors came by trolley, wagon, and automobile. Bands played, outdoor services were held, and everyone knew the war itself was imaginary. For the public, this was spectacle. For many of the men, it may also have felt like the exciting opening act.

But the operation itself was serious, and the novelty did not last. The harder part came after the army moved out. Then it became what it was meant to be: a test of whether thousands of men, horses, vehicles, supplies, and orders could move across real Massachusetts roads under wartime conditions.

A later history of Wakefield’s Richardson Light Guard, one of the units involved, remembered the week as “the most severe and trying ever undertaken by the National Guard of Massachusetts.”

In Billerica, that reality had not fully arrived yet.

Billerica’s role was important, but brief. This was not where the decisive action was expected to happen. It was the rendezvous point and base camp: the place where the Blue Army gathered, organized, drew crowds, and then moved out.

After two nights, the big camp at Billerica disappeared almost as rapidly as it had sprung into position. Clark’s army folded its tents and marched toward Tewksbury, where rain turned the roads to mud and the mock campaign moved on. The first fight went to the Blue Army near Reading…

So no, Billerica did not have a battle — but in July 1911, for one strange weekend, Billerica had an army.

Written by Keith Lavon.
