Billerica’s First Major Leaguer

Mitchell “Mike” Balas

In 1931, a parade of automobiles rolled through Billerica.

It was large enough that some people reportedly thought it was a political celebration. But this was not a campaign event and no visiting dignitary.

It was Billerica sending off a baseball player.

His friends, teammates and others had gathered to honor a young pitcher leaving for a chance with the New York Giants organization. He was given a travel bag and grooming kit. Then the cars took him through town.

The newspapers called him “Mike” Bolaski.

His real name was Mitchell Balas.

You have probably never heard either name.

But you should.

Because before Tom Glavine, before Gary DiSarcina, before Billerica had familiar baseball names attached to it, there was a Howe High School athlete from North Billerica who made it to the major leagues first.

His major-league baseball career formally lasted 1⅓ innings.

His professional baseball career lasted nearly a decade.

Before we get there, though, the name needs explaining, though it is not as confusing as it is sometimes made out to be.

He was Mitchell Balas. That was his legal name, the name on his birth certificate, and the name on his Howe High School diploma.

“Mike” was not especially strange as a nickname. Mitchell seems to have gone by Mike in ordinary life, too. Years later, he signed his name as Mitchell “Mike” Balas.

The unusual part was the last name.

In the local sports pages, especially when he was still a schoolboy athlete around Billerica and Lowell, he was called “Mike” Bolaski.

That was not a simple newspaper mistake.

It was a nickname — one that began on local ballfields and eventually followed him into professional baseball.

A century later, “Bolaski” looks awkward on the page. But the Lowell Sun’s explanation suggests the name lived in the sound more than the spelling. After Balas developed “a throwing arm and a change of pace,” “the boys” started calling him “Mike” Bolaski.

Spoken aloud, I suspect it may have carried more of the point than the printed version preserves — perhaps something closer to Bowl-out-ski — a name for a pitcher who bowled batters out. “Bowl” was more familiar as a baseball term then than it is now. Balas was Polish-American, and “Bolaski” gave the nickname the shape of a Polish surname.

But that is guesswork. The paper was not explaining a new nickname to readers. It was reminding them of one they already knew. The nickname grew out of some combination of his pitching, his surname, his background, and the local sports-page world around him. However it sounded at first, it stuck.

Some later accounts described Bolaski as a legal-name matter. The local record is more convincing. In the same school year that a newspaper list of Howe High graduates named him Mitchell Balas, the sports pages were already calling the athlete “Mike” Bolaski. The simplest explanation is not a formal name change, but a nickname that traveled farther than nicknames usually do.

And it did travel.

The nickname didn’t stay in the schoolyard or the local sports pages. It followed him into professional baseball, far enough that “Bolaski” appeared in box scores while “Balas” appeared on his contract. That would matter later.

I am going to call him Balas through most of this story. But when the papers call him “Mike” Bolaski — and they often do — they are talking about the same person: Mitchell Balas of North Billerica.

And in the late 1920s, Balas was not just another Howe athlete — he may be Billerica’s first clearly documented schoolboy sports star.

These were the early years of school sports, and Howe High was not some kind of athletic machine. Some sports appeared, disappeared, and reappeared. The coverage was scattered. The teams were often uneven.

But he made Howe worth noticing.

A Lowell Courier-Citizen feature called “Mike Bolaski” of Howe High “one of the most prominent high school players in greater Lowell.”

The article was writing about football. Howe had played Lowell High’s Squad B at Alumni Field, and although Howe lost, Balas “made a whale of a showing.” He was called “easily the most outstanding player on the Billerica eleven.”

The writer added that he would be “a great addition to the Lowell high team,” if only it could somehow be arranged for him to attend the local school. Balas had attended Lowell schools through junior high, so the thought was not outlandish.

Then came the broader assessment.

Besides being a “whale” of a football player, Balas was also “a fine basketball player and a real star in baseball.”

That is the profile of a player who had already broken through the local noise.

It is also a reminder that baseball did not have to become his whole athletic story. In a different decade, or with another path, the football version of Balas might have been the one people remembered. He even coached semipro football during the offseason in the pre-NFL era. He was not a baseball-only athlete who happened to dabble in other sports. He was a local all-around sports figure.

But baseball had the ladder. Baseball had the professional system. Baseball had the scouts, the minor leagues, and the possibility — however narrow — that a kid from Howe High could keep climbing until he reached the majors.

A later account said Balas did not begin at Howe as a pitcher. He joined the baseball team as a catcher and had a hard time breaking through on the mound. But he had speed in his throwing arm. His coach worked on his control and helped him develop what the paper called an “easy curve” — presumably meaning a smooth, natural curveball delivery. His arm got him noticed. Control and the curve made him a pitcher.

From there, the paper said, Balas built himself up. Then came the kind of game that explains why people around Billerica remembered him.

On the afternoon of April 26, 1927, pitching for Howe High against Wilmington, Balas threw a no-hitter in an 18–0 victory. He struck out an incredible 22 batters and helped his own cause with a home run and a triple.

That is not just a good schoolboy box score. That is the kind of performance that turns a high school athlete into a local name.

By 1930, local papers could call him an “ex-Howe high pitching star” without needing to explain the reputation. Another account said that “Mike Bolaski” had pitched his Howe High School team “to many a victory during the scholastic season.”

Balas was born in Lowell and Lowell never entirely forgot that. He had gone to Lowell schools before moving to Billerica at twelve, so there was a real Lowell claim on the early part of his story.

But the baseball claim belonged to Billerica.

One Lowell account put it neatly: although he was born in Centralville and moved to Billerica as a boy, “the enthusiasts in the suburban town insist he is theirs.”

Then the writer added the only necessary ruling:

“No argument.”

That is probably the cleanest way to settle it. Lowell could claim the birthplace. Billerica could claim the ballplayer.

And Billerica had reason to. This was not retroactive pride after he reached the majors. By the late 1920s and early 1930s, Balas was already a local name: the Howe High star who struck out 22 in a no-hitter, the town baseball standout, the pitcher sent off to Giants camp with a parade, and later the returning professional whose name could still draw a crowd when people heard “Bolaski” was pitching.

He was also moving through a wider local baseball world around the same time.

The lines were not as clean as they might sound today. Howe High baseball, town baseball, Lowell-area evening leagues, and semipro competition could overlap. A strong high school player might also appear in serious local games, the way later college players might spend a summer in a competitive amateur league.

In 1927, one paper wrote that Billerica fans, “for the first time since the days of the old Blue Stockings and the famous T. R. & T.’s,” were supporting their baseball team “to a man.”

That was not Howe High baseball. It was town baseball — serious local competition, not casual recreation, and for a player like Balas part of the ladder between school sports and the paid game.

1931 coverage also gives a useful glimpse of his background. Balas was described as the son of working people in North Billerica, living on moderate means, with his father employed at the Boston & Maine Railroad car shops. When he finished grammar school, the paper said, “his parents were in a position to send him through high school.”

That line is easy to pass over, but it says something about the world he came from.

Howe High was public, but for a working family in the 1920s, keeping a son in school still meant something. It meant he was not going directly into full-time work. The phrase — that his parents were “in a position to” — should not necessarily be read as prosperity. It could just as easily suggest sacrifice, priority, or a family determined to give a son opportunities.

After Howe, Balas did not simply become a local legend. He became a professional ballplayer.

His professional career began in 1929 with Brockton in the New England League, and almost immediately it becomes difficult to summarize cleanly. There was nothing clean about it.

Brockton. Lynn. Bridgeport. Binghamton. York. Albany. Chattanooga. Dayton. Richmond. Elmira. Lowell. Scranton. Hartford. Erie. Indianapolis. Birmingham.

The list is exhausting, but that is the point.

Honestly, it may not even be complete.

Balas was not climbing one neat ladder from Billerica to the major leagues. He was moving through baseball’s middle ground.

The numbers match the story.

In the minors, Balas was not a star. Across the seasons that survive in the record, he went 56–73. His run average was a little over five. There is no hidden major-league great buried in the numbers.

But he also threw more than 1,000 professional innings.

That is not the record of a player passing briefly through organized baseball. That is a pitcher who spent years earning another start, another contract, and another chance.

A pitcher does not keep getting that much work by accident. There is nowhere to hide on the mound. A pitcher gets the ball and has to get hitters out. If he can’t — every batter exposes it. Every inning exposes it. There is no clock to wait out.

So the 1,000 innings matter.

They are the proof that Balas was not a novelty. They are the proof that clubs kept finding use for him, even when they did not find greatness.

His career had real peaks. In 1931 with Bridgeport, he went 11–11 with a 2.90 ERA and threw 202 innings while only 21 years old. In 1937 with Scranton, he went 9–8 with a 3.29 ERA, walked only 25 batters in 123 innings, and pitched well enough to earn another major-league look.

It also had rough years.

His first professional season at 19 with Brockton was hard: 5–14 with a 5.77 ERA. There were releases, demotions, short stays, returns to local ball, and teams that simply stopped existing.

That is why “journeyman” fits him so well.

He was not a failed prospect in the simple sense. He was not a hidden star. He was a working pitcher trying to survive professional baseball in an era when survival itself was unstable.

In 1930, he pitched briefly for Lynn before the New England League disbanded six weeks into the season. Then he moved to Bridgeport in the Eastern League, posting a winning record and helping the Bears to the second-half title.

That Bridgeport season gave him his first major-league look.

The New York Giants — the National League baseball team that later moved west and became today’s San Francisco Giants — had a working arrangement with Bridgeport, and manager John McGraw invited Balas and two other Bridgeport pitchers to spring training in San Antonio in 1931. That was the trip North Billerica celebrated with the parade, the reception, the travel bag, and the grooming kit.

And that belief was not just local fantasy.

The same winter, an Associated Press photograph published nationally showed New York Giants rookies and officials leaving New York for spring training in San Antonio. The caption listed “Mike Bolaski, pitcher” among the rookies. It also carried a wonderfully blunt line about spring training: some of the rookies “may come trundling back later,” but they were starting out full of hope.

That was exactly the world Balas was entering.

The Giants look did not turn into a major-league debut. Contemporary accounts were blunt about that, too. He was a prospect, but raw. He needed more experience. In one exhibition appearance with the Giants’ reserve team, he was hit hard in relief. By the time the Giants’ second team reached Bridgeport in April, Balas stayed there to pitch for the Bears again.

That became the pattern.

He was good enough to keep getting looks. He was not good enough to make those looks permanent.

Balas was a working professional pitcher. He kept getting the ball. He was useful enough for another club, another manager, or another old baseball man to think he was worth one more start, one more relief appearance, one more contract, one more chance.

He was too good to disappear. He was not quite good enough to make teams stop looking for someone better.

That hard line describes a real baseball life.

Everyone at that level had once been the best player somewhere. Balas had been a Howe High star, a Billerica town baseball standout, and a Lowell-area name. In professional baseball, that just made him one more pitcher trying to survive.

What stands out is how long he kept surviving.

The early 1930s made survival harder. This was Depression-era baseball. Leagues folded. Clubs changed affiliations. Rosters shrank. Major-league support for minor-league clubs was uneven. Balas lived inside that instability.

He pitched for Bridgeport in 1931 and 1932, until the Eastern League disbanded in July 1932.

After the league folded he was back in Lowell-area semipro ball, scheduled to pitch for Billerica-Woburn in the Eastern Massachusetts Twi League. The local story called him Billerica-Woburn’s new hurler and said he had returned from Bridgeport. It reminded readers that he had tried out with the New York Giants and had pitched in fast minor leagues “for a number of years.” It described him as a “strapping lad,” carrying more weight than before, with plenty of speed and curves.

The next day, the paper reported that “the announcement that Bolaski was to pitch drew out a large crowd of baseball followers who had seen him in County league battles some years ago on the South common.” The opposing pitcher was Curt Fullerton, who had pitched for the Boston Red Sox from 1921 to 1925. That is useful context for what “semipro” could mean in that era.

This was not a story about a man who had vanished from baseball — it was a story about a baseball man moving between levels.

The 1933 season is especially hard to pin down in a clean, game-by-game way. Balas appears to have moved through several clubs and rosters, but because much of that movement did not produce official game appearances, the paper trail is uneven. He could be with a club, or connected to one, without leaving much of a statistical mark.

What can be said is this: after stops involving Binghamton, Dayton, and Albany, Balas spent about five weeks with the Washington Senators, then one of the American League’s major-league baseball clubs. He was with the team. He wore the uniform. He pitched daily batting practice to major-league hitters.

But he did not appear in an official league game.

Washington sent him back toward the minors, and soon afterward he was part of the baseball movement that carried him through Chattanooga, Richmond, and Elmira.

The line between minor leaguer, semipro pitcher, local star, and major-league hopeful was not fixed for Balas. He crossed it repeatedly.

So Washington counts as a taste of the majors — it does not count as his major-league debut.

By 1936, his organized baseball career seemed to have narrowed again. He was back in Lowell, pitching for the Laurier semipro club. That could have been the end of the story: the old Howe star, still good enough for local ball, no longer moving upward.

But George “Lefty” Tyler still saw enough to recommend him to the Boston organization.

Tyler was not just another local baseball man. He had pitched in the major leagues himself, most notably with the Boston Braves, including the 1914 Miracle Braves, and lived in Lowell.

Tyler is an interesting character in Balas’s story. A later account said Tyler saw “excellent possibilities” in the young pitcher while he was a teenager and gave “Mitchell — or ‘Mike’ — the start and incentive to follow through.”

Years later, it was Tyler who helped give him another opening.

Balas signed into the Boston system and pitched for Scranton in 1937. That season appears to have been one of his better professional years, and it put him back on the edge of the majors.

It also forced the old name problem into the open.

For years, “Mike Bolaski” had followed him from Billerica into professional baseball. But by 1937, the mismatch between Balas on the contract and Bolaski in the box scores had become serious enough that league officials investigated it. Forfeits had even been threatened against Scranton. Once they were satisfied Balas and Bolaski were the same person, they ordered that only Balas be used.

The nickname had traveled as far as it could. Except locally, Bolaski was still stuck around.

By 1938, the correction had already been made. He was no longer just a local pitcher whose nickname had wandered into box scores. He was in the Boston organization as Mitchell Balas, and soon he would be on a major-league roster under that name.

And still, in the Lowell Sun, one item began: “Mike Bolaski, pardon us we mean Balas…”

That small aside is funny, but it is also revealing. The writer knew the official name. The correction had been made. But to local sports readers — the Billerica and Lowell-area baseball world that had followed him for years — he was still Bolaski.

That is the distinction.

Balas was the legal name and, by then, the official baseball name. Bolaski was the local baseball identity: the Howe High, Billerica, Lowell-area sports-page name that had followed him from schoolboy athletics into professional baseball and proved surprisingly hard to kill.

A 1938 “Rookie Roundup” item identified him formally as Mitchell Francis Balas of the Boston Bees, a right-hander. It described him as six feet tall, 195 pounds, twenty-six years old, from Lowell, living in North Billerica, and coming to Boston after going 9–8 in 23 games at Scranton.

Then came the sentence that sums up the whole career to that point:

“Eight years in the minors.”

Eight years in the minors, and now Mitchell Balas was seeking a berth with the Boston Bees.

If that name feels odd, it should.

The Boston Bees were a short-lived name for Boston’s National League baseball team — the same franchise usually known as the Boston Braves. From 1936 through 1940, the club used the Bees name before returning to Braves. The franchise later left Boston and eventually became today’s Atlanta Braves.

Balas made the club.

He was not simply a one-day emergency addition. The Lowell Courier-Citizen ran a photo spread in April 1938 under the headline “Bees’ Boys Back Home, Anxious to Get Season Going.” One caption showed Balas at breakfast at the Hotel Touraine before reporting.

There is even a wild physical footnote. A game-worn 1938 Boston Bees jersey attributed to Balas has surfaced in the memorabilia market. The auction house described it as the only example of that one-year style it had ever seen. Maybe that is collector’s hyperbole. Maybe not. Either way, even though Balas’s official major-league record lasted only 1⅓ innings, the uniform reminds us that he was there for more than four outs.

By 1938, baseball was not in its infancy. The major leagues were already the center of the American sporting world. The names were national. The box scores were daily reading. The line between minor leaguer and major leaguer was understood.

On April 27, 1938, Balas got into a major-league game.

When Casey Stengel, managing the Bees, handed him the ball, everyone knew what that meant.

It meant he had crossed the line.

It was, however, not a storybook debut.

Boston was already trailing Brooklyn, 9–0, when Stengel brought him in with two outs in the eighth inning. Balas got the final out without further damage, then returned for the ninth.

That is where his entire major-league record was made.

Brooklyn scored three runs on three hits in the ninth, though two of the runs were unearned after Boston committed a pair of errors behind him.

He did not lose the game. Boston had already done that.

The Lowell Courier-Citizen thought the box score was too harsh. When “Mike Balas, the Billerica boy,” finally got his shot, the paper wrote, Roy Johnson gave him ragged support in left field. The paper added that Balas “really looked better than the box score would indicate.”

That sounds like hometown mercy — it was also fair.

The major-league record looks harsh because it has no room to breathe. In a normal career, that would have been one rough appearance with poor support behind him. In Balas’s career, it became the whole line.

Four outs.

Three runs.

One earned.

Career ERA: 6.75.

His one major-league appearance should be understood for what it was. Balas did not enter a tight pennant-race duel with the game on the line. He came in for the Boston Bees in a game Brooklyn had already effectively won.

It was mop-up duty in an early-season game.

But it was mop-up duty in the major leagues.

For one afternoon, the Howe High pitcher from Billerica was not trying to reach the majors anymore.

He was in them.

Without that appearance, Balas would still be worth a footnote: a Howe High star, a town baseball favorite, a long-traveling minor-league pitcher, one of those local athletes who was good enough to chase the game farther than most.

But the one appearance with Boston makes him something else.

It makes him a major leaguer.

Soon after, Boston sent Balas to Hartford in the Eastern League. He went 3–8, then was demoted to Erie in the Middle Atlantic League. When Erie changed hands, Balas became part of the Cincinnati Reds organization. He spent time with Indianapolis and Birmingham before the Reds released him in 1940.

That was the end of his climb.

For Billerica, that would make him the town’s first major-league baseball player by many decades.

The later names are far better remembered, and for good reason. Tom Glavine became one of the greatest pitchers of his generation. Gary DiSarcina had a long major-league career. Their places in Billerica sports memory are secure.

Balas is different.

He was not a star. He was not some hidden great. He was not robbed of a Hall of Fame career. His record is too uneven for that, and there is no need to pretend otherwise.

But he was not a novelty either.

He was a journeyman’s journeyman: the kind of pitcher who stayed useful by staying available, stayed known by keeping himself in the game, and stayed in the game because somebody, somewhere, kept handing him the ball.

The major-league line obscures the larger story. As a major-league pitcher, Mitchell Balas is a footnote. As a professional baseball player, he was not. As a Billerica athlete, he was something more still: a Howe High standout, a local sports-page fixture, a working baseball professional from a working-class family, and the first man from town to cross into the major leagues.

Balas was not lost to baseball history. His name is still there if you know where to look. He died in 1996 in Westford.

But local memory works differently. It tends to keep the stars, the long careers, the clean stories, and the names that are easiest to repeat. Balas left something harder to hold onto: four major-league outs, a strange nickname, a long minor-league trail, and a life that did not remain simple after baseball.

That should not erase what came before.

His major-league career was brief.

His baseball life was not.

Historical Footnote

Some may wonder why Mitchell Balas seems to be entirely absent from local memory despite being Billerica’s first major-league baseball player. I wondered the same. The answer likely has less to do with his baseball career than with what came afterward.

During World War II, Balas was convicted of violating the Selective Service Act and sentenced to federal prison. Contemporary records show that his wartime views and public statements went well beyond what most Americans — including many conscientious objectors — found acceptable. Whatever goodwill he had earned locally as a ballplayer appears to have been badly damaged.

The article focuses on the athlete because that is the subject of the story. The later chapter is real, documented, and important, but it is a different story. Recognizing Balas’s place in Billerica baseball history does not require ignoring what came later, nor does acknowledging what came later erase what he accomplished on the field.
