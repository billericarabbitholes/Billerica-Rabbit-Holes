Dr. Solomon G. Hajjar

Hajjar Elementary School

It has been part of Billerica for more than 50 years, but Hajjar is not the kind of name you expect to see on a school in suburban Boston—and like many names here, it’s one we recognize without always stopping to ask who it belongs to.

Dr. Solomon Gantous Hajjar was born in Lawrence in 1907, the son of Syrian immigrant parents. At the turn of the twentieth century, “Syrian” referred broadly to immigrants from the Levant — what is today Lebanon, Syria, Palestine, and surrounding regions — many of whom, like the Hajjars, were Arab Christians. The family was part of Lawrence’s Melkite community, an Eastern Catholic tradition that was fully Catholic, but culturally distinct.

The census records that the family arrived in the United States in 1899, and for a time lived in South Dakota, where the eldest was born.

By the time Solomon was born, the family was firmly rooted in Lawrence, a flourishing but deeply troubled textile city — in the neighborhood often referred to as “Little Syria,” within the area known locally as the Plains. By 1910, more than 2,000 Syrians lived in Lawrence, making it one of the largest Arab communities in the United States.

It was a dense, highly localized world. Syrian families clustered within a tight network of streets — Elm, Oak, Chestnut, and the surrounding blocks — in closely built tenements and storefront neighborhoods. But it was not isolated. The city shifted block by block. One resident later recalled that “further down from Lawrence Street there was all Italians… up Hampshire Street it was strictly Lebanese… then Tremont Street, that was predominantly Irish.”

The Bread and Roses Strike of 1912 unfolded when Solomon was five years old.

His father operated a grocery store in the heart of Little Syria, placing the family within the commercial life of the neighborhood rather than the factory floor. It was still a working-class world, but a different one — rooted in small business, daily interaction, and a degree of independence.

In the early 1900s, many children left school as soon as they could — often by fourteen — to contribute to family income. Hajjar did not.

He stayed in school, and then kept going.

As a teenager, Hajjar was already visible in public life. Newspaper articles from the period show him everywhere — organizing events, speaking in front of crowds, and serving in leadership roles. He was active in the St. Joseph’s Debating Society and the Young Men’s Association, eventually becoming president. At Lawrence High School, he was a standout debater, won a debating scholarship, and delivered the principal address at the school’s debating banquet.

This was not incidental participation. It was visibility. His portrait appeared in the newspaper multiple times.

In the same city where many left school early, he was standing in front of rooms, making arguments, and being recognized for it.

For a standout student from Lawrence’s Melkite Catholic community, Boston College was a natural next step. It offered not just higher education, but a Catholic institutional world that would have felt familiar.

He continued along that path at St. Louis University School of Medicine in Missouri, another major Jesuit institution.

His path out of Little Syria was upward and connected. The geography changed — from Lawrence to Boston to St. Louis — but the institutional world around him remained consistent: Catholic, structured, and oriented toward professional life.

In 1939, he married Elizabeth J. Morris of Lowell, and together they began building a family in North Billerica.

By 1940, Solomon was already settled into his life’s work — a practicing doctor, a husband, and a father with the first of five children already at home.

By 1940, the world had become unsettled again.

Even before Pearl Harbor, draft preparations for war were underway in the United States. Hajjar served on the Selective Service Board, part of the local structure responsible for evaluating and classifying men for military service.

He did not belong to the small prewar army, but within months of the attack on Pearl Harbor, Hajjar entered military service — likely through direct commission as a physician — and was headed overseas.

He went to war not as a young man finding himself, but as a married, established doctor with two children at home who already knew who he was — and had to make sense of a world that did not.

His wartime service record is incomplete — like many of his generation, portions were lost in the 1973 National Personnel Records Center fire.

What survives most vividly from that period is something unexpected: a privately printed family volume of Hajjar’s poetry, later donated to the Billerica library.

The poems show that writing was not some occasional whim.

In one poem to his wife, he recalled her promise:

“I’ll never cease, until I see
The Lord return you home to me.”

Another, written to his young daughter, closes simply:

“With a father’s most profound and devoted love — Daddy
Somewhere in No. Africa. Tuesday — April 6 — 1943.”

Other poems capture the details of daily life — makeshift tables, improvised camp arrangements, a favored pipe, the quiet routines that filled the spaces between urgency and exhaustion.

And one poem, “My Arab Friend,” hints at something else entirely.

Hajjar, the son of Syrian immigrants raised in Lawrence’s Melkite community, suddenly found himself in North Africa — in a place that may not have been entirely foreign to his imagination.

He begins:

“While strolling here in desert land
And holding poppies in my hand
An Arab, old, and lame and weak
Approached and spoke in manner meek.
“Salaam Alaikum,” he did say
His wrinkled face bore lines so gay
Somehow he knew, I’d comprehend
This, “Peace to you – my Yankee friend.””

Then, in a brief but revealing moment, he writes:

“And unexpectedly, I said
‘Allah Maikum,’ and touched my head
His eyes bulged wide, his smile did beam
And truly happy he did seem.
And grinning now from ear to ear

He touched his heart, as he drew near,

And stretching out his sun tanned hand
We greeted each on Arab land.”

The poem continues. It is a small interaction, but a telling one.

Surviving records also show that Hajjar received the Legion of Merit, a serious wartime decoration for exceptionally meritorious service.

Later paperwork ties him to the 161st Medical Battalion of the U.S. Fifth Army in the war’s final phase in Italy, suggesting the surviving record captures more of the end of his service than the beginning.

In 1945, at the war’s end, he was discharged as a Captain for permanent disability.

In the years after World War II, Hajjar built a remarkably broad medical career. Records place him in a wide range of institutional and civic roles.

His work extended beyond Billerica as well. In 1959, he was appointed by Massachusetts Governor Foster Furcolo to the state’s Board of Registration in Medicine, a body responsible for overseeing the licensing and conduct of physicians.

Hajjar also served as Associate Medical Examiner for the 5th Middlesex District, a role that brought him into some of the most difficult moments a community can face. Newspaper reports from the time occasionally note simply that “Dr. S. G. Hajjar was called to the scene,” where he would determine the cause of death — quiet, matter-of-fact lines that hint at work that was anything but routine.

But through all of it, one place keeps coming up: the Billerica schools.

In a career that moved across institutions, the schools were the constant—and what he was best known for.

Hajjar served as a school physician from 1953 until his death in 1966, at just 59 years old.

When a new school was proposed later in 1966, the decision followed naturally. He had already been part of daily life for years.

A town historian later wrote that Hajjar had “established a close relationship with the townspeople, making himself readily available for house calls in time of need.”

When Hajjar Elementary was formally dedicated on October 18, 1970, members of his family, along with friends and town officials, gathered for the ceremony.

Hajjar is a fascinating character — a Lawrence kid from an immigrant family, raised in the Plains, a standout student at a time when that alone was an achievement, a Boston College graduate, a St. Louis-trained physician, and a decorated wartime Army doctor whose own poems survive from the field — but above all, a familiar and trusted presence in Billerica.

Written by Keith Lavon.
