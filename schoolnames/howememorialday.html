Dr. Zadok Howe’s Memorial Day Tradition

A School Tradition at South Cemetery

One old Billerica school tradition caught my eye during my school series research.

For many years, the junior high school class decorated the grave of Dr. Zadok Howe in South Cemetery each Memorial Day.

At first glance, it feels a little unusual. Howe was not a veteran. He died in 1851, a decade before the Civil War and before Decoration Day even existed.

What sent me down the rabbit hole was really the oddness of it.

The more I looked into it, the more it seemed tied to an older version of Memorial Day — closer to Decoration Day — when schools, cemeteries, flowers, veterans, speeches, and civic memory all blended together. Grave decoration itself long predated the Civil War. Memorial Day did not invent the practice so much as formalize it around the memory of Union war dead.

Howe’s connection was educational rather than military. His bequest helped support high school education in Billerica for generations. In 1940, Superintendent Eugene C. Vining wrote:

“Custom makes it a grateful service for the Junior Class of the high school to decorate Dr. Howe’s grave in the South Cemetery each Memorial Day.”

Even Vining’s wording feels a little defensive, as though the tradition already needed some explanation by 1940. And if the custom faded after World War II, maybe that is not surprising. Memorial Day had always been rooted in military remembrance, but after another generation of dead soldiers, there may simply have been less room for a school tradition honoring a non-veteran benefactor, even one as important to Billerica as Howe.

And honestly, that probably is not a bad thing. Today the tradition feels like it belongs somewhere else — maybe the opening of the school year or some other moment connected directly to education rather than military remembrance.

Still, I find it fascinating that generations of Billerica students once marked the day this way.

Written by Keith Lavon.
