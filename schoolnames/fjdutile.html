Frederick J. Dutile

The field came first.

What came first — the school or the field? In Dutile’s case, it was the field. And the name that came with it. The River Pines “playground” — a term used at the time for what was essentially an open recreation field, similar to what is still technically called Akeson Playground in Pinehurst — first formally appears in the town records by 1950. In 1952, the first season of Billerica Little League, it was already being used for baseball. In the years that followed, the field was never simply finished and left alone. Committees were formed, money was requested, and improvements continued even as the field was already in use. Fred Dutile appears in that story not only through many years of service on the field committee, but also through Billerica Little League, where as a founder and later president he repeatedly went directly to Town Meeting, successfully seeking financial support for equipment and improvements like backstops. By the time the school project took shape, the ground already carried years of civic effort — and Dutile had been part of much of that work. Frederick Joseph Dutile was born in 1914 in the remote hamlet of North Hudson, New York. He was the son of a French-Canadian family, raised in North Billerica after his family arrived in 1919, when Fred was four years old. His father, Joseph, was an immigrant; his mother, Rebecca Bolia, though of French-Canadian origin, was born in the United States. The part of Billerica they moved to was shaped not by farms, but by the railroad. His father worked as a railroad carpenter and car repairman — the kind of work that brought families like theirs to town. The neighborhood around them was made up of similar working families. The Dutile family arrived in Billerica just as the Boston & Maine Railroad’s Billerica Repair Shops were in full operation. Today, the site is largely hidden from view and impossible to access, but at the time it would have been impossible to ignore. It was a vast, 553-acre, round-the-clock operation built not for people, but for locomotives and boxcars. Tracks, steam, noise, and constant motion defined the area, and thousands of workers moved through it each day. We don’t always think of Billerica as a railroad town. In the long view, its time as a major railroad center was relatively brief. But in the 1920s, North Billerica was about as close to a classic New England railroad-and-mill town as you could find. The shops were operating at full scale. The mills were near their peak. The area moved with an intensity that’s hard to picture today. The family rented their home, as many did in North Billerica — first on Pollard Street, later on Lawrence Street. Their home was a step removed from the cramped apartments of Lowell, but the old farm world that still defined much of Billerica would have felt just as distant. The name itself has an interesting story. “Dutile” is of French origin with strong Quebec roots, and in earlier records appears with variations such as Dutel, Dutill, and Dutelle. Even his first name shifts slightly in the records — recorded as Frederic on his baptism, later appearing as Frederick in official documents, most often Fred in newspapers and town records, and remembered simply as Freddy by those who knew him. At some point, the pronunciation of Dutile shifted — though whether that happened over time or was already in place by his generation is unclear — and today the name is often said quite differently than its French roots would suggest. The French-Canadian population in this area, centered around Little Canada in Lowell, was not insignificant. Newspapers were published locally in French — including the daily L’Etoile, which ran until 1957 — serving a deeply rooted French-Canadian community. In the records, upon arrival the Dutile family were members of Saint Andrew’s Church in North Billerica, lived among other French-Canadian families — many of them railroaders — and maintained close ties within that community. For Fred’s generation — born in America, but often raised in households where French was still spoken — this meant living between two worlds without fully belonging to either. The records that might tell us the most about his early life — the French-language press and parish archives — are not easily accessible today. Publications like L’Etoile are only beginning to be digitized. If the Dutiles appeared in those pages, those mentions would have been visible to one community at the time, but largely invisible to others — and remain difficult to recover now. Work came early for Fred in the aftermath of the Great Depression. By the age of 15, he was no longer in school and was working as a farm laborer. By then, his father had also left the railroad and was using his carpentry skills in house building — part of the next great building boom in Billerica. Fred would follow a similar path. By 1940, Fred had married Rosemary Dugan. They would have five children. The Dutiles had moved to the River Pines neighborhood of West Billerica — already in the area he would spend the rest of his life serving — and was working as a truck driver for a coal delivery company. The coal dealer in North Billerica was Dutile Coal, and it was operated by his uncle, George Dutile. During World War II, Dutile served in the U.S. Navy aboard the submarine USS Toro, joining at its commissioning in 1944 and taking part in Pacific patrols in the final months of the war. Some of these patrols included lifeguard duty — surfacing in contested waters to rescue downed airmen. It was dangerous work: nearly one in ten submarines lost during the war went down on lifeguard assignments. After returning home, Dutile became part of the generation that helped build modern Billerica. As president of D & D Construction Company — Dutile & Dionne — he worked on local projects and was personally involved in the work itself. In one town report, he is specifically thanked for excellent, above-and-beyond work on the Bennett Public Library. He appears most often in the records through his involvement in community life — as a Town Meeting Member, on local committees, and through youth sports. That involvement extended into parish life as well. Dutile was a charter member of the Billerica Knights of Columbus and served on committees and in leadership roles within his parish. Those who knew him remembered something simpler: his constant smile. In January of 1963, Fred Dutile died suddenly at just 48 years old. The Town Meeting that March opened with a moment of silence — just two months after his death. Later that same meeting, Article 14 was approved, naming the River Pines field the Frederick J. Dutile Memorial Playground. The naming of the field and the planning of a new elementary school were unfolding at the same time, but not as part of a single coordinated plan. Dutile Field stood before the first shovel hit the ground for the school. But as the school rose beside it, the name was already there — it may not have taken much more than a simple suggestion for it to carry forward. It reflects something already true by then — Dutile’s name was closely tied to that place and that neighborhood. Dutile’s name did not come from long-held land or inherited legacy, but from a specific place and a specific time in the town’s history. He spent years helping build that space for the children of River Pines and West Billerica. The school that stands there today serves those same children.
