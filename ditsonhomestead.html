The Ditson Homestead

Thomas Ditson’s Front Step

You likely know the story of Thomas Ditson — more specifically Thomas Ditson Jr. Maybe you or your kids went to Thomas Ditson Elementary School, or had friends who did. Thomas Ditson, and especially what happened to him in 1775, is well documented.

But did you know the front step from his house still survives outside the elementary school? And have you ever wondered where he actually lived?

I did.

So...

The Ditson Homestead once stretched from the town line to the Shawsheen River along Boston Road. It included a two-story farmhouse with an attached barn. The “last house in Billerica” was later described as “dignified and substantial,” and the overall homestead as “magnificent” and “immense.” Four generations of Ditsons lived on this farm for almost a century. It is thought the farmhouse was built around 1733, with Thomas Ditson Jr. assumed to have been born there in 1741.

The buildings were lost to fire sometime between 1911 and 1915.

Based on deed descriptions, surveys, and the known photograph of the house, I believe the homestead appears to have stood on Boston Road just south of Riverbank Terrace, between Riverbank Terrace and Adelman Street.

I would not call this “lost” information. Into the early 20th century, the location was still known and discussed, and maybe others still know. But I do not think it is widely known today.

A fun story follows.

Older Billerica histories note that the Ditson Homestead stood at what is known as Harnden’s Corner — so called because the Harnden family farmed the land from the 1850s until 1911, when it was sold for development. That development became Riverbank Terrace, following another successful development on the opposite side of Boston Road.

Based on the survey, it even appears that the original intent may have been to retain the house and barn, though for what purpose is unknown.

Unfortunately, the buildings were lost to fire before 1915. Based on the town’s Annual Report, I suspect the likely date was May 3, 1912, when a fire was reported at a house owned by A. Adelman — the legal owner as developer — with estimated damage of nearly $5,000, no small amount at the time.

The last Harnden to live on the property was Catherine Harnden, who never married and ultimately sold the place. She died four days after the fire destroyed her longtime home. She had already sold the property, and development was already underway, so the fire clearly did not cause the sale or her direct death — but the timing is striking. The farm and fire were important enough to be specifically mentioned in the newspaper notice of her death.

Then things get even more interesting.

In 1934, a Special Town Meeting was called to consider just four items:

Would the town accept the doorstep of the birthplace of Thomas Ditson?

Would the town transfer money to inscribe the doorstep?

Would the town rename Pinehurst School as Ditson School?

Would the town fund a historical marker at Harnden’s Corner?

Oh, to have been at that meeting.

The Finance Committee did not recommend the articles, and the town’s Annual Report notes that the debate included “several bursts of oratory, participated in by several citizens.”

In the end, the town agreed to accept the doorstep at no expense to the town and approved $50 from Surplus Revenue to furnish a cast-iron plate — a compromise, since the originally proposed grading appropriation had been rejected. According to the Annual Report, only $30 was ultimately spent.

Apparently that was enough for one night.

The school-renaming question and the site-marker question were both postponed indefinitely, and the meeting adjourned.

The naming question returned in January 1935 at the Annual Town Meeting. Article 11 asked whether Pinehurst School should be renamed Thomas Ditson School. After a motion to dismiss failed, the article passed, and the school received the Ditson name.

The marker proposal, however, seems never to have returned.

And so, nearly a century later, the step survives with its plaque, even being moved to the new school site; the school carries his name; but the actual site of the homestead remains unmarked.

Written by Keith Lavon.
