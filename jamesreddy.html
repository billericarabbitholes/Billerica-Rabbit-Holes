James Reddy Beneath the Ice

Reddy Square and the Rescue at Nutting Lake

Earlier this year, I helped Billerica’s Veterans Services office research the names attached to the town’s memorial squares.

There were many names on the town’s list. Many stories, many lives.

But one name stuck with me — James Reddy.

Reddy Square was the one I kept coming back to because it was different.

James Reddy was born in Flatrock, Newfoundland on January 20, 1887. During World War I, he enlisted in the U.S. Army and served as a wagoner with the Supply Company, 39th Infantry, 4th Division.

He was part of the critical logistical system that supplied and moved an army, and contemporary accounts repeatedly identified him as a World War veteran. He also became a U.S. citizen during the war through the military naturalization process available to servicemen.

But that was not the reason Town Meeting gave for creating Reddy Square.

It was created because of the tragedy that took place on February 13, 1937.

After several days of moderate weather, the ice on Nutting Lake had become dangerously weak. That Saturday afternoon, two emergencies unfolded within moments of one another at different points on the lake. Contemporary reports differed somewhat on the exact sequence.

Near a landing off Lake Street — identified in one newspaper as Foley’s Landing — James Reddy broke through the ice with two young sisters: about two-year-old Joan Ann Gibbons and four-year-old Mary Gibbons.

Reddy managed to push both girls toward relatively firm ice before collapsing and disappearing beneath the water. A group of Howe High School basketball players, alerted by their screams, reached the scene and pulled the girls to safety. The fire chief later said Reddy had “undoubtedly saved the lives of the two Gibbons youngsters.”

At roughly the same time, Frank D. Lupo, a 23-year-old man from Cambridge, broke through the ice near the Turnpike road side of the lake, about 150 feet from shore. One newspaper reported that he fell through while skating; another said he had heard the children’s cries and was moving toward them when the ice gave way.

The girls survived.

Reddy did not.

Neither did Frank Lupo.

Police later said that seven people had been in the water during the unfolding emergencies. Police and firefighters recovered the two bodies with grappling irons. Several of the young men who had attempted rescues were treated for immersion and exposure. The fire chief was called away from Town Meeting to direct the operation.

The reaction around Nutting Lake was immediate. Residents were reportedly “up in arms” and began pushing for lifesaving equipment to be placed at different points around the lake. The newspaper suggested that both deaths might have been prevented had proper equipment been available.

The town responded.

At the 1938 Town Meeting, Article 22 authorized the placement of six life preservers around Nutting Lake.

Town Meeting then approved Article 23, formally naming the junction of Lexington Road and Pond Lane “James Reddy Square,” in honor of:

“a hero who gave his life to save the lives of two children in Nuttings Lake.”

Pond Lane was already commonly known as Lake Street — the name used in contemporary newspaper accounts — and it was formally renamed Lake Street within the next two years. Today, the square stands at the intersection of Lexington Road and Lake Street.

James Reddy might have received a memorial square as a World War I veteran. Many of Billerica’s memorial squares honor veterans for their service.

But when Town Meeting voted to create Reddy Square, it did not leave the reason to inference. The article did not speak generally of military service. It recorded a particular act and expressly called him a hero for it.

Reddy Square was the second memorial square created in Billerica. The first honored Isaac Hurd, a Revolutionary War figure.

I do not think that makes Reddy more worthy of remembrance than the veterans honored elsewhere around town, but it is different.

The town did more than preserve his name. It told us exactly why it wanted him remembered.

And the story did not end beneath the ice.

Joan Ann and Mary grew up, graduated from Howe High School, married, had children, and lived long lives, though both eventually moved away from Billerica.

I had hoped to end this story at James Reddy’s grave.

A 1938 government application requested a military headstone for his previously unmarked grave and identified Oak Grove Cemetery in Medford as his burial place. Yet Oak Grove’s detailed records contain no burial for James Reddy, and the cemetery’s superintendent searched the World War I lot and confirmed that no trace of him could be found there.

For now, his final resting place remains unknown.

But what is known is that Reddy did not save an abstraction that afternoon. He saved two children, two entire futures, and everything that followed from them.

Reddy Square preserves the memory of a World War I veteran who used his last strength to save Joan Ann and Mary Gibbons from drowning beneath the ice.

A Note on the Name

Lowell newspaper accounts printed the surname as “Ready,” and that spelling also entered some early town paperwork. Records identify him as James Reddy. The discrepancy was noticed by the Town Clerk when the square was voted on, and the memorial sign uses the corrected spelling.

Written by Keith Lavon.
