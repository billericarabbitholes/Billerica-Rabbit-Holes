The Bell Billerica Broke

July 4, 1842

On July 4, 1842, Billerica celebrated Independence Day by ringing the town bell so hard that it cracked.

Henry A. Hazen recorded the story in his 1883 History of Billerica, the town’s standard early history. His exact wording is worth keeping: the bell was “cracked” by “violent ringing” on July 4, 1842.

Hazen did not say the bell merely “cracked” while being rung. Whatever happened, he understood it as something more than ordinary bell ringing gone wrong.

In 1842, this was not just a loss for local history. The meetinghouse bell was still a working part of town life — used to summon people, mark public occasions, and carry its sound across the community. Billerica had not just damaged something old. It had broken something the town still needed.

It is a little comical from a distance: the town celebrated the Fourth so hard that it broke its own bell. But at the time, it was probably also embarrassing and expensive. A cracked bell could no longer do its job, and replacing it was not a small matter.

Ringing the town bell for the Fourth was not strange. Nineteenth-century Independence Day celebrations were loud affairs — bells, cannon fire, speeches, dinners, bonfires, and fireworks all belonged to the day. Fireworks existed, but the holiday had not yet moved into the modern form of organized municipal fireworks display.

Hazen does not tell us whether Billerica’s 1842 ringing was formally organized or simply the work of a few overenthusiastic people with access to the rope. Either way, Billerica — or at least whoever had hold of the bell rope — took it too far.

Surprisingly little of the bell’s history was known with certainty, and not for lack of trying.

Hazen was writing only about forty years after the accident. He searched the town records and consulted residents who remembered the bell. Even with those advantages, he could reconstruct only part of its story.

Billerica’s first town bell dated to the early eighteenth century. In 1711, the town voted to use money from the sale of land west of the Concord River to purchase a bell for the meetinghouse. A few years later, men were hired to construct the stairs, platform, door, and ironwork needed to hang it “fit to ring.”

That first bell remained in service until 1753. The town then ordered it taken down, carried to Boston, and sold toward the purchase of a replacement. The new bell was to come from England and weigh no more than 500 pounds. Space for six additional pews inside the meetinghouse was also sold to help finance it.

After that, the record becomes less certain.

Hazen wrote that this second bell was “probably the same” one cracked by violent ringing on July 4, 1842.

Probably.

With little in the records, Hazen turned to the memories of older residents who had known the broken bell. They recalled that its inscription included the name “Billericay,” using the spelling of the English town.

That detail strongly suggests the bell had been made specifically for Billerica, and it also helped create a more romantic local tradition: that the bell had been presented to Billerica by Billericay, England.

Hazen did not seem to believe it.

The records showed that Billerica had purchased its bells. He also reasoned that a formal gift between the two towns would almost certainly have left some record on one side of the Atlantic or the other. His best explanation was simpler: the town ordered a bell from an English maker, and the founder rendered the Massachusetts town’s name in the familiar English form, “Billericay.”

The town soon replaced the broken bell with a new one cast by Revere & Son of Boston. That bell has its own story, and later generations would know it far better.

But this is not the story of the Revere bell.

It is the story of the one before it: the bell Hazen tried to identify, the bell older residents remembered as bearing the name “Billericay,” and the bell that was rung so hard in Billerica that it broke.

Written by Keith Lavon.
