Dahliaitis

George L. Fish and Hav-a-Look Gardens

“I’ve had several kinds of fevers in my existence,” South Billerica flower grower George L. Fish wrote in the 1920s. “Bird Fever, Hen Fever, and Dog Fever, as well as Flower Fever; but the worst of all is the Dahlia Fever.”

Fish called it “Dahliaitis.”

“Once it fastens its tentacles upon you,” he warned, “you are a goner — no cure!”

A century later, that may sound exaggerated. But Fish was not alone. In the 1910s and 1920s, flowers occupied a much more visible public place than they usually do today. Railroad stations held garden and decoration contests. Horticultural societies drew serious competitors. Newspapers reported on prize blooms.

Dahlias fit perfectly into that world. New varieties were named, judged, praised, ordered, and argued over. Growers competed for size, color, stem strength, form, and novelty.

From South Billerica, Fish entered that world — and briefly rose near the top of it.

George Leslie Fish was born in New Bedford in 1860 and came from the historic Fish family of Sandwich. By the early 1920s, he had established Hav-a-Look Gardens in South Billerica along the Concord River, in that corner of town near Bedford where practical geography often mattered more than municipal boundaries. The catalogs consistently listed the business as “So. Billerica, Mass.” while using a Bedford post office address.

This was not a typical nursery operation selling fruit trees and vegetable seeds.

Fish described himself as a “Dahlia Specialist — Gladioli Enthusiast.” His real interest was breeding new exhibition flowers: massive decorative dahlias with elaborate names, unusual colors, and carefully judged forms intended for flower-show competition.

The names alone sounded theatrical: Hav-a-Look Dazzler, Queen of the Autumn, Noontide, Glorious Sunrise, Pond Lily, Mrs. George A. Sexton of Hav-a-Look, and Frances Cooper of Hav-a-Look.

Fish wrote about them with almost obsessive precision. Some were praised for “looking right at you” on exhibition stems. Others were valued for petal twists, unusual coloration, or enormous bloom size. One flower, Hav-a-Look Dazzler, was described as “the peer of all red dahlias.” Another, Hav-a-Look Gem, featured petals of “bright vermilion shading to orange and changing again to henna with age.”

And this was not merely local enthusiasm.

Fish exhibited at the combined Massachusetts Horticultural Society and Dahlia Society of New England shows in Boston, where his flowers repeatedly won medals and first prizes. In 1925, Frances Cooper of Hav-a-Look captured the Gold Medal Sweepstakes for best seedling in the show. Newspaper coverage from the period identified Fish as president of the society, placing the South Billerica grower among the leading figures in New England’s dahlia culture.

The scale of the operation is also surprising. In one catalog, Fish explained that Hav-a-Look Gardens raised “a thousand or more Dahlias” from seed each year, though only a small fraction were retained for future trial. The rest — including unnamed seedlings and occasionally even mislabeled stock after tags were lost during handling — became part of Hav-a-Look’s inexpensive “Surprise Box” offerings.

That detail says a great deal about what Hav-a-Look really was. This was not a rigid commercial nursery planned from the beginning. It was a breeder’s obsession that gradually became too large not to sell.

Fish eventually said so himself. Hav-a-Look Gardens, he explained later, had been “conducted more as a hobby, than as a commercial business.” Like many hobbies, it had “outgrown its location.”

By March 25, 1927, Hav-a-Look Gardens had formally incorporated in Westport, Massachusetts, carrying the name and stock away from Billerica. The new site on Pine Hill Road promised more room, new buildings, and a larger future. Fish’s growing work moved south, closer to his old coastal Massachusetts roots, and the South Billerica chapter came to an end.

It was an ambitious moment — and perhaps an unlucky one.

The company was trying to turn a prize-winning hobby into a larger commercial operation just as the country was moving toward the Depression. By 1930, Hav-a-Look could still point proudly to Fish’s record: he had grown choice dahlias since 1916, originated seedlings, won prizes and medals at the Massachusetts Horticultural Society and Dahlia Society of New England shows, and served as president of the Dahlia Society of New England in 1924 and 1925. Yet even then, the brochures still described Hav-a-Look as something that had grown organically from hobby into business rather than the other way around.

But after that, the trail thins.

The surviving catalog photographs from the Billerica years show carefully maintained garden paths, flower beds, exhibition displays, and sprawling fields surrounding the Fish property. Visitors were explicitly welcomed to the grounds. The name “Hav-a-Look Gardens” feels less accidental. The place was meant to be seen.

Today, little remains locally beyond catalogs, photographs, deeds, and scattered references. The Billerica flowers themselves appear to be long gone. Named dahlias survive only if the same tuber line keeps being divided and grown year after year. Once that chain breaks, the flower disappears, even if its name remains in old catalogs.

Still, with historic dahlias there is always the faint possibility that a variety survives somewhere under an old label, a forgotten garden, or a renamed stock line. The flowers themselves may be gone, but the catalogs survived. Preserved by libraries and agricultural archives, they offer a vivid glimpse into a short-lived but surprisingly serious South Billerica flower world.

For a few years in the 1920s, George L. Fish caught “Dahliaitis,” raised more flowers than South Billerica could contain, and turned a garden along the Concord River into one of New England’s prize-winning horticultural operations.

Written by Keith Lavon.
